1What is the difference between population and sample?
Population is the full set you want to study; a sample is a subset drawn from the population used for analysis.
2What is a parameter vs. a statistic?
A parameter describes a population (e.g., μ, σ); a statistic describes a sample (e.g., x̄, s).
3What is the Law of Large Numbers?
As sample size increases, the sample mean converges to the population mean.
4What is the Central Limit Theorem (CLT)?
CLT: the sampling distribution of the sample mean approaches normal as n grows, regardless of population distribution (given finite variance).
5Descriptive vs. Inferential Statistics?
Descriptive summarizes data (mean, median, charts); inferential makes conclusions about populations using samples (tests, CIs).
6Variance vs. Standard Deviation?
Variance is the average squared deviation; standard deviation is its square root, in the original units.
7What is a probability distribution?
A function describing the probabilities of outcomes of a random variable (PMF for discrete, PDF for continuous).
8Difference between PMF and PDF?
PMF (probability mass function) for discrete variables gives exact probabilities; PDF (probability density function) for continuous gives densities; probabilities are integrals over intervals.
9What is a cumulative distribution function (CDF)?
CDF gives the probability that a random variable is ≤ x; it is the integral (or sum) of the PDF/PMF up to x.
10What is expected value (expectation)?
The weighted average of all possible values of a random variable, using their probabilities (mean of the distribution).
11How is variance defined?
Variance = E[(X − μ)²]; sample variance uses denominator (n−1) for an unbiased estimator.
12What is covariance?
Covariance measures joint variability of two variables; positive means they move together, negative means opposite.
13What is correlation?
Correlation standardizes covariance to [-1,1], measuring linear association strength (Pearson correlation most common).
14Pearson vs Spearman correlation?
Pearson measures linear relationships; Spearman is rank-based and captures monotonic associations, robust to non-linearity/outliers.
15What is conditional probability?
P(A|B) is the probability of A given B occurred: P(A∩B)/P(B).
16What is Bayes' theorem?
P(A|B)=P(B|A)P(A)/P(B): updates prior beliefs P(A) with evidence P(B|A) to get posterior P(A|B).
17What does independence of events mean?
Events A and B are independent if P(A∩B)=P(A)P(B); knowledge of one doesn't change the other's probability.
18What is mutual exclusivity?
Mutually exclusive events cannot occur together: P(A∩B)=0 (e.g., rolling 2 and 3 on same die roll).
19Common discrete probability distributions?
Binomial, Poisson, Geometric, Negative Binomial, Multinomial.
20Common continuous probability distributions?
Normal, Uniform, Exponential, Gamma, Beta, Student's t.
21What is the Bernoulli distribution?
A single-trial binary distribution with probability p of success (0/1 outcomes).
22What is the Binomial distribution?
Distribution of number of successes in n independent Bernoulli trials with success probability p.
23What is the Poisson distribution used for?
Modeling counts of rare events in a fixed interval with rate λ (e.g., number of emails per hour).
24What is the Exponential distribution?
Models time between independent Poisson events; memoryless property: P(T>t+s|T>t)=P(T>s).
25What is the Uniform distribution?
All values in an interval are equally likely (continuous) or equal probability among discrete outcomes.
26What is the Normal (Gaussian) distribution?
A symmetric bell-shaped distribution defined by mean μ and variance σ²; common due to CLT.
27What is the Standard Normal distribution?
A Normal distribution with mean 0 and variance 1 (Z-distribution); used to standardize variables.
28What is a z-score?
Number of standard deviations an observation is from the mean: z=(x−μ)/σ.
29What is Student's t-distribution?
Like Normal but with heavier tails; used when sample size is small and population variance unknown.
30When to use a t-test?
To compare means when population variance is unknown, especially for small samples (one-sample, two-sample, paired variants).
31What is a confidence interval?
Range of values, derived from sample data, likely to contain the population parameter with specified confidence (e.g., 95%).
32Interpretation of a 95% confidence interval?
If you repeated sampling many times, ~95% of constructed intervals would contain the true parameter (not that there's 95% probability for a fixed interval).
33What is hypothesis testing?
Framework to test claims about population parameters using sample data: set null/alternative, compute test statistic, compare to threshold/p-value.
34What is a p-value?
Probability of observing data at least as extreme as observed under the null hypothesis; small p-value suggests rejecting null.
35Type I vs Type II error?
Type I: false positive (reject true null); Type II: false negative (fail to reject false null). Power = 1 − Type II error.
36What is statistical power?
Probability of correctly rejecting a false null (detecting effect); increases with effect size, sample size, and α.
37What is effect size?
A measure of the magnitude of a phenomenon (e.g., Cohen's d for mean differences, r for correlations), important beyond statistical significance.
38What is ANOVA?
Analysis of Variance compares means across more than two groups by partitioning variance into between- and within-group components.
39When to use ANOVA instead of multiple t-tests?
Use ANOVA to avoid inflated Type I error when comparing 3+ groups; use post-hoc tests to find pairwise differences.
40What is the Chi-square test?
A test for association between categorical variables (Chi-square test of independence) or goodness-of-fit to expected frequencies.
41What is a goodness-of-fit test?
Assesses whether observed data match a specified distribution (e.g., chi-square goodness-of-fit).
42What are non-parametric tests?
Tests that don't assume specific distributional forms (e.g., Mann–Whitney U, Wilcoxon signed-rank, Kruskal–Wallis).
43What is bootstrapping?
Resampling with replacement to estimate sampling distributions, standard errors, and confidence intervals without strong parametric assumptions.
44What is resampling?
Techniques that repeatedly draw samples from data (bootstrap, permutation tests, cross-validation) to assess variability or significance.
45What is a permutation test?
A non-parametric test that computes the sampling distribution of a statistic by permuting labels to evaluate significance.
46What is regression analysis?
Modeling relationship between dependent and independent variables; linear regression models linear relationships; many other variants exist.
47What is the method of least squares?
Estimates regression coefficients by minimizing the sum of squared residuals (differences between observed and predicted values).
48What is R-squared?
Proportion of variance in the dependent variable explained by the model; ranges 0–1; adjusted R² accounts for number of predictors.
49What is adjusted R-squared?
R² adjusted for number of predictors and sample size; penalizes adding irrelevant variables.
50What is multicollinearity?
High correlation among predictors causing unstable coefficient estimates; detect with VIF and address by removing/combining features or regularization.
51What is the Variance Inflation Factor (VIF)?
VIF quantifies multicollinearity for a predictor; VIF>5–10 often indicates problematic multicollinearity.
52What is heteroscedasticity?
Non-constant variance of residuals across levels of predictors; violates OLS assumptions and affects standard errors; tests: Breusch–Pagan.
53What is autocorrelation?
Correlation of a time series with its lagged values; indicates dependency across time; can bias OLS errors (Durbin–Watson test).
54What is time series analysis?
Analysis of ordered data over time, focusing on trends, seasonality, autocorrelation, forecasting using ARIMA, exponential smoothing, etc.
55What is stationarity in time series?
A stationary series has constant mean, variance, and autocovariance structure over time; many models assume stationarity (use differencing if needed).
56What is ARIMA?
Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average combines AR(p), differencing (I(d)), and MA(q) for modeling and forecasting stationary/non-stationary series.
57What is exponential smoothing?
Forecasting method that weights recent observations more heavily (Simple, Holt's, Holt–Winters for seasonality).
58What is cross-validation?
Technique to evaluate model generalization by splitting data into folds (k-fold, leave-one-out) and averaging validation performance.
59What is stratified sampling?
Sampling ensuring representation across strata (groups) proportionally or equally to reduce sampling variability for subgroup analyses.
60What is cluster sampling?
Sampling by selecting whole clusters (e.g., schools) randomly and then sampling within; used when population is naturally grouped.
61What is sampling bias?
Systematic error from non-representative samples (selection bias) leading to invalid inferences about population.
62What is non-response bias?
Bias when participants who do not respond differ systematically from responders, affecting survey estimates.
63What are missing data mechanisms?
MCAR (missing completely at random), MAR (missing at random), MNAR (missing not at random); determines appropriate handling strategies.
64How to handle missing data?
Options: deletion, imputation (mean, median, regression, MICE), model-based methods; choice depends on mechanism and amount missing.
65What is MICE?
Multiple Imputation by Chained Equations: iterative method filling missing values with multiple plausible imputations to reflect uncertainty.
66What is an ROC curve?
Receiver Operating Characteristic plots TPR vs FPR across thresholds; area under curve (AUC) measures classification discrimination.
67What is the Precision-Recall curve?
Plots precision vs recall across thresholds; more informative than ROC for imbalanced data focusing on positive class.
68What is a confusion matrix?
Table of actual vs predicted class counts: TP, FP, TN, FN; used to compute precision, recall, specificity, etc.
69What is specificity?
True Negative Rate: TN/(TN+FP), measures correctly identified negatives.
70What is sensitivity (recall)?
True Positive Rate: TP/(TP+FN), measures correctly identified positives.
71What is False Discovery Rate (FDR)?
Proportion of false positives among positive calls (FP / (TP+FP)); important in multiple testing contexts.
72What is the multiple testing problem?
Conducting many hypothesis tests increases chance of Type I errors; control with Bonferroni, BH (FDR) procedures.
73What is the Bonferroni correction?
Adjust significance α by dividing by number of tests (α/m) to control family-wise error rate conservatively.
74What is the Benjamini–Hochberg procedure?
A method controlling False Discovery Rate (FDR) less conservatively than Bonferroni, ranking p-values and selecting threshold.
75How do outliers affect statistical analysis?
They can skew means, inflate variance, bias estimates; consider robust measures (median, trimmed mean) or transform/remove after investigation.
76What does robustness mean in statistics?
Insensitivity of statistical methods to deviations from assumptions or to outliers (e.g., using median vs mean).
77Why apply log transformation?
To reduce skewness, stabilize variance, and convert multiplicative relationships to additive ones for modeling.
78Normalization vs Standardization?
Normalization rescales to [0,1]; standardization transforms to zero mean and unit variance (z-scores).
79What is the Kaplan–Meier estimator?
Non-parametric estimator of survival function accounting for censored data; plots survival probability over time.
80What is the Cox proportional hazards model?
Semi-parametric model for time-to-event data relating covariates to hazard; assumes proportional hazards over time.
81What is the likelihood function?
Function of parameters given observed data; maximum likelihood estimates (MLE) maximize this function.
82What is Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)?
Method to estimate parameters by maximizing the likelihood of observed data under the model.
83What is Bayesian inference?
Framework combining prior beliefs with data (likelihood) using Bayes' theorem to obtain posterior distributions over parameters.
84What are prior and posterior distributions?
Prior: belief about parameter before seeing data. Posterior: updated belief after observing data, proportional to prior × likelihood.
85What is a conjugate prior?
A prior whose posterior is in the same family as the prior, simplifying Bayesian updating (e.g., Beta prior for Binomial likelihood).
86What is a Bayesian credible interval?
Interval containing parameter values with specified posterior probability (e.g., 95% credible interval ↔ 95% posterior probability).
87What is the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm?
Iterative method for MLE with latent variables: E-step computes expected sufficient stats given current params, M-step maximizes likelihood.
88What is a Hidden Markov Model (HMM)?
Probabilistic model with hidden states following a Markov chain and observed emissions dependent on those states, used in sequence modeling.
89What is the Markov property?
Future state depends only on the present state, not on the full history (memoryless property of Markov processes).
90What is a Bayesian network?
Directed acyclic graph representing conditional dependencies among variables with associated conditional probability tables.
91What is a Gaussian Process?
A collection of random variables where any finite set has a joint Gaussian distribution; used for non-parametric regression and uncertainty quantification.
92What is Kernel Density Estimation (KDE)?
Non-parametric method to estimate a continuous probability density by smoothing data points with a kernel function (bandwidth choice matters).
93What is a Bayes factor?
Ratio of marginal likelihoods of two competing models: evidence in favor of one model over another in Bayesian model comparison.
94What is model selection?
Choosing among models based on criteria (AIC, BIC, cross-validation) balancing fit and complexity to avoid overfitting.
95What are AIC and BIC?
Information criteria: AIC (Akaike) and BIC (Bayesian) penalize likelihood by complexity; lower values indicate preferred models, BIC penalizes complexity more strongly.
96What is residual analysis?
Examining residuals (observed − predicted) to check model assumptions: normality, homoscedasticity, independence, and to detect outliers.
97What is robust statistics?
Methods less sensitive to outliers or assumption violations (e.g., median, trimmed mean, M-estimators).
98Statistical significance vs practical significance?
Statistical significance indicates low probability of result under null; practical significance assesses whether the effect size is meaningful in real-world terms.
99What is reproducibility in statistical analysis?
Ability to re-run analysis and get same results by documenting data, code, environment, seeds, and random splits; critical for scientific validity.
100How should one learn statistics effectively?
Combine theory (probability, inference) with practice: analyze real datasets, implement methods, simulate scenarios, and interpret results critically.
